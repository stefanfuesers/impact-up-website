Impact Up's Origins — Philosophical Background

An impact never stands alone in the world. It interacts with the world – just like other impacts do. Impact is our way into the work, close to the people and the projects; we want to improve the interplay between impacts. We want to network and bring together more, and look more towards possible cooperations for the transformations within systems. And we want to do this systemically – trusting that the greatest resources for it lie with you, too.

Why Impact Up

Thinking onward from impact.

In the standard impact logic, impact is the top step – the point where a piece of work demonstrably has an effect on society. The impact staircase, shaped above all by PHINEO, ends here. Our name says how we think onward: Impact Up. We don't accept impact as the highest level – we think impact beyond it, up the staircase.

There, a network opens up: many impacts meet each other, and between them arises what we work with – coherence of impacts, complementarity of impacts, systemic analysis in search of the next impact. Impacts can also run counter to each other; that is exactly when it pays to clarify concerns together and find solutions.

The phase transition

When many impacts interlock.

That needs an image of its own: the single staircase becomes a field. The view shifts from the step to the connection – from the height of one initiative to what arises between many.

The single impact staircase becomes a field of many impacts: each peak stays recognisable, but the connections create a coherent pattern that no staircase alone produces.

Linear impact logic. The impact staircase thinks of impact as a chain: input leads to output, output to outcome, outcome to impact. That makes the performance of a single initiative traceable and plannable. Its limit lies in staying bound to the single initiative and treating impact as a controllable sequence – in complex fields, where many actors act on one another, this image falls short.

Phase transition. Where many impacts meet, something arises that none of the individual staircases produces on its own. Impact forms between the actors, not in the single step. It runs non-linearly and circularly: results feed back onto their own preconditions, small shifts can amplify.

Field effect. Many impacts become a shared impact once they turn coherent, complementary, and compatible with one another – once what moves some connects to what others do. This is not another step above impact. It is a different question: how a resilient field emerges from many individual peaks.

Why an institute

Theory and practice.

Our work has an epistemological core: how we describe the world shapes what we can see and what becomes available to us as action. We share this constructivist premise with systemic consulting. Ruth Seliger (Systemische Beratung der Gesellschaft, 2022) applies it consistently to society itself: a society in crisis-laden tensions between ecology, economy, and democracy – and we, who work on it, are part of it. It can be changed from within, above all through organisations, because that is where societal action is organised.

That is why we call our theory pages constructs: deliberately built thinking tools that make shared work on impact possible and that we examine and develop further together with you. For us, a construct is good when it opens up action. Methodologically, we work with what carries the question at hand: systemic organisational development in the Milan/Heidelberg tradition with circular questioning, hypothesis-building, reflecting teams and constellations work; causal loop diagrams and impact models from the systems dynamics line; Nonviolent Communication, co-creation or action research; but also a broad methodological repertoire from peace work such as dialogue, conflict transformation and dealing with the past. Which tool, on the basis of which theory, can support the work in practice is decided by the subject at hand.

Theory and practice need each other – there is nothing more practical than a good theory. We bring theory into practice and practice into theory, undogmatically and open in many directions. We call ourselves an institute because that is where theory and practice come together as equals. What matters is what we want together – the methods follow. In this sense, beyond consulting, we also understand ourselves as an organising actor in the system, one that builds networks and takes part.

Where we start

Listening, trust, networking.

Things in the network only come together once people know each other, exchange, and find shared spaces to think in. That's why we start with the basics: listening, letting resonance emerge, letting trust grow between people – and building networks, around the topics and between the topics.

Between what is visible about an organisation and what changes only slowly, there is a middle level: the relationships and power dynamics between the actors. This is where it is decided whether change holds or disappears again. This relational level is our home – methodologically and biographically.

We bring this stance from two sources: from civil peace service, where relationships, power, and conflict dynamics are the central level of work, and from systemic organisational development, which understands systems through their patterns and interactions. Both work in a conflict-sensitive and participatory way, both take power seriously and do not smooth over differences.

With us, the topic and the concern pave the way to the method, not the other way around. We look together with you at your impact and accompany it to where there is space for it – in coherence and complementarity with the impacts of others. For this work there are numerous, diverse approaches from systemic consulting, co-creation, Developmental Evaluation or Systems Change. We want to develop fitting approaches on the challenges themselves rather than setting them in advance. The search starts together with you.

Where this leads

From stance to discipline.

From this follows a stance: we want to contribute – as part of a larger whole, in which one person's impact connects to another's – while staying close to the people and their projects. From this stance grows a professional discipline. We call its overarching concept systemic impact orientation: the craft of thinking impact systemically and aligning initiatives so that many individual impacts can become a shared impact in the field. It has two directions that carry equal weight for us: impact orientation as a core task, shaped systemically – and orientation towards systemic impact in society. It gives this task a precise description and a method – and beneath it stand concrete approaches that can be worked with.

Systemic practice – two distinct approaches that, together, yield much more than the sum

Whoever works systemically today, thinks in systems-theoretical terms, or practises systems thinking, is moving through a field that at first glance looks fragmented – different schools, different vocabularies, different toolkits. On closer look, though, it shows: the strands emerge almost simultaneously, out of the same observation, and they complement each other in practice far better than their separate traditions might suggest.

Read more

In the 1940s and 1950s, almost simultaneously across different disciplines, a shared dissatisfaction with the classical Cartesian-Newtonian worldview takes shape. Western science had specialised in breaking its objects down into ever smaller substances and explaining their behaviour from those substances' properties. This programme was extraordinarily successful on its own terrain – yet ran into limits everywhere it had to grapple with living, learning, developing, cultural or ecological phenomena. A cell is not a sack of molecules. A family is not the sum of its members. A forest is not a warehouse of trees. A market is not the addition of rational actors.

Not substance, but relation

The answer that crystallises – with Bertalanffy in biology, Wiener in cybernetics, Forrester in system dynamics, Bateson in anthropology, Maturana and Varela in cell biology and later Luhmann in sociology – is at its core the same turn: it is not things that are primary, but their interconnections. What we call a "thing" is a stabilised pattern of relations, not a carrier of those relations. Relation produces what first appears to us as a distinguishable thing. This is a Copernican turn whose reach is underestimated, because it runs against our everyday thinking.

Cause and effect as loop

From this follows a second insight that all strands share: if the world consists of interconnections, nothing runs in only one direction. Cause and effect are not arrows but loops. What happens comes back – via detours, delays, other actors – to its starting point and co-constitutes what can happen next. Forrester calls this feedback loop, Maturana and Varela call it autopoiesis, Luhmann operative closure, Bateson second-order cybernetics, Meadows describes it as the interplay of reinforcing and balancing loops. Different languages, the same insight: systems reproduce themselves through circular interconnections.

Change addresses patterns, not culprits

From this follows a third shared consequence: a methodological mistrust of simple causality, simple blame and simple steering. Whoever wants to change something in a complex constellation – in an organisation, a family, an ecological structure, a society – does not get further by looking for culprits or pinpointing "the right" measure. One must understand the pattern. Change becomes a question of where the pattern is open to other unfoldings. Meadows formulates this as leverage points, the Milan school as forming hypotheses about circular patterns one can connect to. In both cases the same move: instead of addressing actors, address the interconnections.

Where the schools specialise

Up to this point, all strands speak with one voice. The difference begins one storey lower and is essentially a matter of specialisation: as much as the field agrees that the world consists of interconnections, the phenomena the various strands work on differ – and so does the material those interconnections are made of, and the methods appropriate to that material.

The communicative layer: hypotheses and circular loops

On one side stand phenomena in which interconnections consist of communication: families, teams, organisations, advisory relationships, societal discourses. What circulates here are expectations, attributions, hypotheses, recognitions, meanings. These loops are real, but not measurable like a water level. One only gets them in view by counting oneself, the observer, into the equation – because every question, every hypothesis changes the system one is trying to understand.

Exactly here, the family-therapy-rooted tradition – Bateson, Palo Alto, the Milan school around Selvini-Palazzoli, and its German translation in the Heidelberg school – has developed its craft. Circular questioning, hypothesis-building, reframing, reflecting teams, constellations work: methods for working in self-referential systems without trying to steer them. They respect that the system operates by its own logic, and work on connectability rather than imposition.

The material layer: feedback loops and impact models

On the other side stand phenomena in which interconnections consist of material and energy flows, chains of effect and institutional structures: matter cycles, energy budgets, supply chains, climate systems, economic accumulations. These flows act whether or not someone understands them or attaches meaning to them. CO₂ acts in the greenhouse independently of the discourse about it. A population collapses when its food base collapses – independently of the story told about it.

Here, the systems dynamics tradition – Forrester, Meadows, Senge and today's sustainability and systems-change research – has developed its craft. Causal loop diagrams, stocks and flows, delayed feedbacks, non-linear thresholds, leverage points. Tools that achieve something the communicative tradition cannot achieve from within itself: modelling material dynamics over long time horizons and large scales, taking them seriously in their own logic.

One programme, two toolkits

The apparent opposition between the two lines dissolves once one grasps that they are not arguing about the world, but about different layers of the same phenomenon. Both state the same fundamental: look at the interconnections, not at the substances. They differ only in their answer to what is interconnected in their domain – and which methodology is appropriate to that material.

Systems thinking is the observation that the world consists of patterns of stabilised interconnection, not of substances. Which kind of interconnection carries in each case – communication, meaning, trust, hypotheses on the one hand; matter flow, energy flow, ecological effect on the other – is an empirical question of the given subject.

Most of the phenomena we really work on – cities, conflicts, economies, health systems, climate policies, transformations of every kind – are anyway constituted simultaneously in both layers. They have a communicative logic of their own that cannot be derived from material conditions; and a material logic of their own that cannot be reframed into compliance. Both layers interact constantly, but they cannot be dissolved into each other. Whoever looks at only one will systematically underestimate the other.

Exactly here it shows why connecting the two approaches yields more than their sum: hypothesis work and circular loops need the structural sharpness of feedback loops and impact models to take hold in sustainability and transformation fields. Systems thinking needs the methodological craft of systemic practice to become genuinely connectable at the points where change has to pass through people and organisations. Only together does a practice emerge that is up to its subject.

The Three Fields of Impact Up

↻ Drag to rotate
Impact Up – the Institute for Systems Change – seeks to engage and shape with you through three central entry points in the system:

Earth as Ecosystem

The Earth is an ecosystem with planetary boundaries that also limit economic growth and challenge justice on this planet in many layered ways. We begin the process of (re-)integrating human life harmoniously into the Earth's ecosystem.

Peace & Non-Violence

Non-violence is the foundation of human dignity and of peace between people. Structural and cultural non-violence is an important – though often invisible – part of it. We begin the process of becoming more aware of violence in its various forms, and of transforming conflicts constructively.

Social Participation

Social participation is the foundation of communal life. Everyone needs participation, an orientation toward the common good, and individual security – for example through a basic income. We begin the process of shaping social participation as the foundation of democracy and social cohesion, and putting humanity at the centre.

These are just three of many possible entry points. They feel central and important to us, which is why we want to start with you here. We step into existing systems, where other entry points exist as well. What we offer is an initial orientation, so we can identify together the approaches to constructive change and use them co-creatively. Where we end up emerges in the process with you. The Ideal-Typical Process

From impact to systems change.
Your impact can do much more.

Here we sketch the ideal-typical process from impact to Systems Change. Impact doesn't merely want to be aimed at – it wants to be clearly formulated, brought into resonance with the team, attuned with others, and embedded in the field. What we mean by each is unfolded in the four steps.

01

inside

Orientation through
Resonance

We believe that impact directed inward can do a great deal of good. Meaning in one's own work emerges through impact – this is the strongest motivation there is. When all colleagues or project participants find their own sense of purpose in resonance with the impact, this also provides the orientation that good teamwork needs. As the first step in the process, we begin shaping the impact so that it can support everyone's work.

02

inside

Coherence through
Structure

A concrete impact that gives a team orientation only works for one project, one department, or another small unit. But internal structures are often larger: many projects in a programme, many departments in an organisation, or other configurations. Impact can orient inward, but on the outside it needs an overarching impact logic that gives focus to several teams – which leads to cooperation rather than competition. As the second step in the process, we begin building the structures of the different impacts so that larger units of cooperation form, and a whole organisation can act as one. That strengthens cohesion and success – less friction, clearer alignment, a whole that is more than the sum of its parts.

03

outside

Coherence in the
Field

When an entire organisation or programme has shaped its own impact so that all participants find meaning in it, much has already been achieved. And yet: it is easy to pursue two good impacts side by side that simply don't function in coherence and complementarity. On the contrary – when actors in a neighbourhood or thematic field don't coordinate, they may unintentionally work against each other or reinforce existing power imbalances. As the third step in the process, we begin to consciously embed your impact into the field: Who works alongside you? Who picks up where you leave off? Where does power shift when you take a clear position? What does it take for everyone in the system to first move in the same direction? The basic precondition for Systems Change.

04

outside

Systems Change
Facilitation

When actors in a field already work side by side, the next step is for them to work with each other – not just coherently, but in shared movement. This doesn't happen on its own. It needs someone to hold the space in which different logics, mandates and power relations can find each other without flattening one another out. As the fourth step in the process, we facilitate such spaces – networked, power-aware, with the patience that relationships of substance demand. We work with what emerges: shared mental models, new practices, shifting resource flows. Systems Change doesn't happen because of facilitation, but it becomes more likely when someone holds the process. That's our role here.

From many such processes, we hope Systems Change will emerge – and we'd be glad if your process becomes part of Impact Up, and Impact Up becomes part of yours.

Our Philosophy

Impact Up – Institute for Systems Change

We make space for your impact.

We want to work with people who want to make a difference – who want to shape the world into something better and more communal. We know our way around this kind of impact orientation, but we are not alone in that. We exist because we see a clear gap.

Impact describes the social change triggered by a project. But when many such changes meet and accumulate, they no longer act independently – they bring forth a shared impact that, in a dynamic world, becomes the new starting point. So one should aspire to make a meaningful contribution to the whole. That requires Systems Change.

In the German-speaking world this aspiration is barely there. Impact measurement, evaluation logics, project tools – everything ends at impact. How many impacts add up to a shared whole is a question that is hardly asked here. In the English-speaking discourse it is. There it's clear: Systems Change is not plannable, often invisible – but its conditions can be named: stable relationships, shared context, shifted power. That is exactly where we want to start with Impact Up – as a German-speaking Institute for Systems Change.

We want to work practically together with you toward Systems Change. How we understand it – with the Six Conditions of Kania/Kramer/Senge, with Rayner and Bonnici's Context, Connection, Power, with the practices of the School of System Change – you can find in detail on the Systems Change → page.

·

From the Civil Peace Service and from systemic organisational development, we bring exactly the breadth of methods that matters for Systems Change – methods that carry relationships, integrate power dynamics, take conflicts seriously and work context-specifically across heterogeneous constellations of actors. Conflict-sensitive. Participatory. It is precisely on that middle layer – relationships and power – that it is decided whether change holds systemically or gets lost. That is our home, methodologically and biographically.

The topic and the question shape the path to the method, not the other way around.

We want to bring impact to the centre, calibrated to context, focused on power and connection, and simply make a contribution.

·

In practice this means: we look at your impact together with you. And then we accompany it to where there is space for it – into networks, into power-aware spaces, into connection and coherence with other impacts. That makes Systems Change more likely. Where it fits, we also facilitate what emerges from it.

But we are not the ones who make Systems Change. We walk the path together with you, see what shows up, and help shape it. Systems Change does not happen through us – it happens with those who inhabit the system.

Who we are

This site is written in the "we", because Impact Up is an idea that can only emerge in community. That communal sense is part of Impact Up – the Institute for Systems Change – from the start. We launch the idea with this page and build Impact Up step by step together with you.

For now, we are not primarily looking for clients here, but for process initiators we accompany with Impact Up – or the other way around. The two are often interchangeable. There is, for example, the Agency for Shaping the Future, which we accompany and which accompanies us; the Co-Creators Circle, where Impact Up is part of the conversation; and a larger process with UBIE.

We always understand ourselves as part of something larger.

We call ourselves an institute – but not in the sense of a university department, with expertise as a commodity. In systemic work, no one can really steer anyway – which expert happens to join in is often secondary. What counts is that someone brings breadth, and someone asks: "Let me ask xy – we'll figure it out." We see Impact Up as an organising actor within the system, not as a consultancy that simply offers services. We build networks in many directions – and above all, we want to be part of it.

Which also means: you are warmly welcome to join in. Just say how you'd like to be part of it: info@impact-up.org.

Stefan founded Impact Up – the Institute for Systems Change. Most recently he spent six years as Advisor for Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation in the Civil Peace Service of GIZ. His work consisted of countless workshops, meetings and trainings with equally varied planning, learning, development, exchange and coordination needs. Out of annual project planning with eight diverse partner organisations, he co-shaped organisational development, helped grow and strengthen networks, and brought people together in the way they happened to need to come together. His training as a yoga teacher and as a practice-group leader for Nonviolent Communication keeps flowing into such workshops.

After returning to Germany in 2025, Stefan had time and appetite to extend his practice and reflect on it. He is currently training in systemic organisational development to work as a certified systemic practitioner – and has found a great deal of resonance for his own work in the literature on Systems Change and co-creation. He brings the methods of planning, monitoring, reflection and evaluation, together with the methods of peace work – dialogue, conflict transformation, dealing with the past and trauma – into the systemic training. Within the Institute for Systems Change, he develops approaches for impact work in purpose-driven networks. Clarity is never imposed, at most articulated as a goal of the process work and shaped together. Stefan brings methodological breadth, a network to expand it further, and prepares the methodology context-specifically and co-creatively with whoever sits across from him. Added to this are the humanity, empathy and sensitivity it takes to truly listen, see and feel what is at stake.

Stefan studied International Business, European Studies and International Studies in four countries; his work experience in further countries adds to that, allowing him to develop his qualities interculturally in practice. That is why he doesn't want Impact Up to remain only in German, but to make it available in English and Spanish as well. To make that happen, he has recently engaged intensively with AI – and gladly brings these skills along, so that we can together reach the Systems Change we see as essential.

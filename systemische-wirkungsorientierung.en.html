Systemic Impact Orientation

Systemic impact orientation has two directions: shaping impact orientation – planning, monitoring, evaluation – systemically, and, the second direction, orienting towards impact that emerges in the field and changes the system itself – in other words, pursuing systemic impact. Impact Up uses both: we believe that the orientation as well as the impact itself can be improved systemically. We work with many systemic methods – and in our work on systemic impact we draw strongly on the Systems Change literature, which places great value on networks.

The What

Systemic impact: transformation of society.

The what of our work is systemic impact: impact that changes the system itself – all the way to the transformation of society. We pursue this path through more networking, more exchange, more coordination, so that many individual impacts can become a shared impact in the field – coherent, complementary, and compatible with one another. Tools along this path are systems maps, which make the field visible, and approaches such as Systems Change, which also bring elements of orientation with them.

Systemic impact comes from working at the system – in Donella Meadows's sense, at the leverage points where reinforcing and balancing feedback loops converge and small shifts can move great things.

We call this second gaze impact coherence in the field: many impacts become effective together once they turn coherent, complementary and compatible with one another – once what moves some connects to what others do. This runs non-linearly and circularly: results feed back onto their own preconditions, small shifts can amplify – effects the systems-thinking tradition in monitoring and evaluation has described for some time. Co-creative field work, as Jascha Rohr describes it, maps this field again and again anew and works with what shifts within it.

The How

Systemic work: stance and craft.

Systemic work begins with a stance: curiosity instead of judgement, interest instead of diagnosis, appreciation towards everyone involved. It assumes that the solutions to a concern are already present within the system itself – as resources, experiences and ideas that often stay invisible in everyday life; consulting activates this potential. Whoever accompanies from the outside holds multi-partiality in the process: equal distance to all perspectives and decisions. This outside perspective – second-order observation – makes visible what often lies in the system's own blind spot: recurring patterns in the interplay, not just individual content. Listening and resonance come first – only once a system feels heard does it become open to a new view of itself.

Impact orientation is established practice in the non-profit sector and speaks many languages: Theory of Change, Outcome Mapping, the impact logic based on the IOOI model, and – in peace work – Reflecting on Peace Practice. We use them as craft tools – which logic fits is decided by the initiative. This holds for every approach we bring: it lives from the input of those it concerns – systemic consulting from the system itself, co-creation from those involved, Systems Change from the Primary Actors. The important content comes from the collaboration; the theory builds on it.

Planning, monitoring and evaluation are umbrella terms for countless, very different activities. With them we shape content – and the way we do it makes the difference: circular rather than only linear, with feedback loops, with second-order observation, with impact knowledge that emerges together with those involved.

With Ruth Seliger we understand change as something that succeeds from within the system, above all through organisations: our process stance begins where organisations observe and interpret their own impact themselves. The existing impact logic that an initiative brings with it remains usable – we extend it with exactly this process stance.

Impact reflection

How we look at impact with you.

Here the first direction becomes concrete. Together with you we look at your impact: we formulate it, plan on its basis, develop teams and structures along it – and keep coming back into conversation about what has actually happened. Impact in complex fields runs in circles and takes time; some of it stays invisible for long stretches. That is part of it – we hold it together and trace it. How you present your impact to the outside remains in your hands; we strengthen the view inwards and into the field.

In planning we work with a Theory of Change as an adaptable frame: it holds assumptions and impact pathways, and it moves along when the field changes. In reflection we begin with what can be observed – with changes in behaviour, in relationships and in practices – and work back from there to our own contribution. Approaches such as Outcome Harvesting or Developmental Evaluation have shaped this way of working; for us it is simply shared impact reflection.

For this looking, the systemic tradition hands us three lenses – Bob Williams and Richard Hummelbrunner put them like this:

Boundaries ask what limits an initiative draws: who and what belongs to the system under consideration, what remains outside. Interrelationships direct the gaze to the interactions that carry a system, and to the feedback loops between them. Perspectives keep open from whose point of view impact is interpreted and which voices are heard. Together they make the complexity of a field discussable without reducing it to a single number.

The Umbrella Term

Many methods, one orientation.

Systemic impact orientation is the umbrella term under which we gather many of these methods – we coin the term, and that is how we mean it. We unfold Systems Change with its own deep logic on its own page.

Sources

What we draw on.

  • PHINEO — Kursbuch Wirkung (IOOI-Logik). phineo.org Established impact logic: Input–Output–Outcome–Impact.
  • DGSF — Systemische Organisationsberatung und Organisationsentwicklung. dgsf.org A stance of respect, multi-partiality and appreciation; resources and potential for change are activated within the system itself, not supplied from outside.
  • von Schlippe, A. & Schweitzer, J. — Lehrbuch der systemischen Therapie und Beratung. Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht. vr-elibrary.de Foundational work on multi-partiality, resource orientation and second-order observation in systemic consulting.
  • Meadows, D. — Thinking in Systems: A Primer. Chelsea Green, 2008. donellameadows.org Foundations of systems thinking: stocks, flows, reinforcing and balancing feedback loops – and the leverage points: places in a system where small shifts carry great change.
  • Williams, B. & Hummelbrunner, R. — Systems Concepts in Action: A Practitioner's Toolkit. Stanford University Press, 2010. bobwilliams.co.nz Three core systems concepts: Interrelationships, Perspectives, Boundaries – the basis of the three lenses.
  • Patton, M. Q. — Developmental Evaluation. Guilford Press, 2010. betterevaluation.org Ongoing, adaptive impact reflection in dynamic systems; non-linearity and emergence as the normal case.
  • Rayner, C. & Bonnici, F. — The Systems Work of Social Change. Oxford University Press, 2021. global.oup.com Context, Connection, Power as relational levels of practice; bridge to the Systems Change page.
  • Seliger, R. — Systemische Beratung der Gesellschaft. Carl-Auer, 2022. carl-auer.de Systemic logic for societal change: constructivism, crisis conditions between ecology, economy and democracy; change succeeds from within the system – above all through organisations.
  • Rohr, J. — Die große Kokreation. Murmann, 2023. murmann-verlag.de Field process theory with the phases resonance – transformation – co-creation (emergence) – cultivation; co-creative mapping of the field as change work.

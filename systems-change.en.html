Why now

Systems Change?

Systems Change has established itself in the English-speaking world over the past decade and has not really arrived in the German-speaking one. Systems Change in English means the active – though never fully plannable – transformation of a system.

Definitions of Systems Change

A systems-change approach is one in which decision-makers take the entire system into view – including all its interconnected parts – along with its effects on inaction and inequalities.

It rests on the conviction that a change in one part of the system can (sometimes unpredictably) affect what happens elsewhere.

It argues for tackling the complexity of inequalities and inaction together – as a whole system – rather than having individual parts of the system attempt this in isolation.

— leadingthemovement.org

We understand Systems Change as the reconfiguration of a system – including its constituent parts and the interactions between them – such that a new system emerges that behaves in a qualitatively different way.

— systemschangelab.org

One step beyond impact

The question behind Systems Change.

Impact is, in the prevailing logic of effect, the highest rung – the point at which one's own work demonstrably has societal consequences. Whoever achieves and documents impact has, in the sense of today's German effect-measurement, reached the goal.

Systems Change doesn't simply add another rung on top. It asks a different question: How do many individual impacts mesh in such a way that, together, they bring about a phase shift in the field? How do impacts become complementary, coherent, connectable to one another – instead of running side by side, or unintentionally against one another? This question has been established in English-language discourse for about a decade – in German consultancy work, it has methodologically not yet arrived.

This is exactly where we work: at the place where one actor's impact enters productive relation with the impacts of others. Looking inward into the carrying capacity of one's own impact, thinking onward together with others, weaving outward into networks – and facilitating where actors are wise enough to attune themselves to each other.

Six Conditions

Where Systems Change takes hold.

Six conditions on three layers of depth. The deeper, the more powerful, the more invisible. (after Kania, Kramer & Senge · FSG)

Structural – what becomes visible when we look at an organisation or a field: which policies apply, which practices are established, where the resources flow. This is where consultancy often steps in – with mixed results, because changes at this level remain reversible as long as the layers underneath don't move with them.

Relational – the half-visible depth: in which relationships actors stand, who has power where, whose impact can connect to whose. This is where it is decided whether structural changes hold, or are rolled back. This is our home – methodologically and biographically, from systemic organisational development and from years of practice in peacebuilding, where relationships, power and conflict dynamics are the central working layer.

Transformational – the most invisible, slowest layer: the mental models with which actors interpret reality. They rarely change directly. They shift when experiences on the upper layers call them into question. We don't primarily work here – but we orient our work so that it becomes connectable to such mental shifts.

In practice

What this looks like in our work.

Out of this theory, we have shaped an ideal-typical process in four steps – from anchoring one's own impact, through inner coherence, to coherence in the field and the facilitation of shared movement. You'll find this process in detail on the homepage, along with the three offers in which we make it practically accessible.

In their book Systems Work of Social Change, Rayner and Bonnici condense the work on the relational layer into three terms: Context, Connection, Power. Whoever takes Systems Change seriously works with what is actually happening in the given context, changes relationships, and shifts power. That is exactly our approach: context-specific and power-critical, we place impact, meaning and purpose at the centre – with a breadth of methods around it that brings them to their full potential.

Such a breadth is, for example, gathered by the School of System Change in ten practices: making systems visible, working at multiple levels, recognising connections and interactions, including diverse perspectives, working with activating and resisting forces, thinking in time scales, recognising patterns, embracing complexity, questioning assumptions.

Methodologically, we work with what carries the question at hand: systemic organisational development in the Milan/Heidelberg tradition with circular questioning, hypothesis-building, reflecting teams and constellations work; causal loop diagrams and impact models from the systems dynamics line; Nonviolent Communication, co-creation and action-research formats; and the methodological repertoire of peace work, dialogue, conflict transformation and dealing with the past. Which tool fits is decided by the subject – not by a school.
